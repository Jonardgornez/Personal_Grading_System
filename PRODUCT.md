# Product

## Register

product

## Users

NORSU faculty members (college/university teachers) using GradeSync as an internal institutional tool. They return to the app frequently during the semester — not onboarding cold. Primary tasks per session: recording attendance, entering scores, checking grade standing, and reviewing analytics. Tech comfort is mixed; some are digital-native, others are not. They are time-constrained: sessions between classes, not leisurely browsing.

## Product Purpose

GradeSync is a centralized grading platform for NORSU teachers. It replaces spreadsheets and paper records with a single place to manage subjects, enroll students, track attendance, record activity scores, configure grading weights, compute final grades, and review class analytics. Success means a teacher can open the app, complete a grading or attendance task, and close it — with confidence the data is correct and the interface got out of the way.

## Brand Personality

Professional and trustworthy. Calm, institutional, reliable. Teachers should feel they are using a tool that was built with care for their specific workflow — not a generic SaaS dashboard duct-taped to an education context. Three words: **Reliable. Focused. Respectful.**

Tone is quiet confidence. Not loud or flashy. Not stiff or corporate. The interface should feel like a well-made instrument — predictable, clear, and durable.

## Anti-references

- Legacy enterprise software (dense gray forms, SharePoint-era UI, government portal aesthetics — the product should feel modern without being trendy)
- Consumer social apps (no gamification, no bright playful palette, no feed-style interactions)
- Generic warm-neutral AI scaffold (no cream/paper backgrounds, tracked-caps eyebrows on every section, identical card grids)

## Design Principles

1. **Data clarity over decoration** — grades, attendance rates, and pass/fail stats are the content. Visual polish should amplify them, not compete.
2. **Predictable over clever** — teachers returning daily rely on muscle memory. Interactions should work the same way everywhere; surprises erode trust.
3. **Earn each element's place** — information density is a feature for this audience, but clutter is a liability. Every element on screen should answer a question the teacher actually has.
4. **Feedback at every action** — loading, saving, error, success: the system always tells the teacher what happened. They need to trust that their input was recorded.
5. **Structured complexity** — grading formulas, weight configurations, and session management are genuinely complex tasks. The interface should scaffold them step by step, not flatten them into one overwhelming view.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA compliance. Prioritize color contrast (4.5:1 for body text), keyboard navigability for all primary flows, and screen-reader-friendly form labels. Teachers may use this on institutional computers with older browsers; avoid cutting-edge CSS that lacks broad support. No reduced-motion animations by default; provide `prefers-reduced-motion` fallbacks for all transitions.
